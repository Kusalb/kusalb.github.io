Adelaide's Quiet AI Revolution: How South Australia Became a Serious Contender
Lot Fourteen sounds like a real-estate listing. But walk through the old Royal Adelaide Hospital site on North Terrace today and you'll find Google, Amazon, Accenture, and a growing cluster of deep-tech startups — all orbiting the University of Adelaide's Australian Institute for Machine Learning, the largest university ML research group in Australia. SA punches well above its weight. In February 2026, the SA Government announced a major new funding injection into AIML — recruiting international AI talent, funding PhD scholarships, and scaling applied work with local SMEs. The AIML Industrial AI SME Grant Program (launched June 2025) is funding manufacturers, agtech businesses, and aged-care providers to embed AI into real operations. The Responsible AI Research (RAIR) Centre — launched December 2024, a collaboration between AIML, CSIRO's Data61 and the SA Government — focuses on misinformation, safe AI in physical environments, diverse AI systems, and explainability. Adelaide's job market is reflecting the shift: demand for ML engineers, AI agent developers, and data strategists is accelerating across commercial and government sectors. What makes SA interesting isn't just research pedigree — it's the density. Academia, defence, and startups share the same suburb. That intimacy breeds applied innovation.

LLMs / Research
Chain-of-Thought, RAG, and the Messy Reality of Getting LLMs to Do Maths
Everyone who has spent time prompting large language models has hit the same wall: confident reasoning, beautiful structure, wrong arithmetic. My research with Dr. Savitha Sam Abraham at the University of Adelaide tackled this head-on — testing Chain-of-Thought prompting, few-shot examples, and RAG against the GSM8K benchmark using LLaMA. Chain-of-Thought forces step-by-step reasoning before committing to an answer, catching semantic errors before they cascade — but it doesn't fix calculation errors. Few-shot examples anchor the model to a format and help with problem interpretation, but gains plateau quickly past five or six examples. RAG was the most interesting: by retrieving similar solved problems as context at inference time, we reduced both semantic confusion and structural errors. The latency cost is real, but for high-stakes reasoning tasks, the accuracy gains justify it. The broader takeaway isn't about GSM8K. It's about the gap between "the model can do this" and "the model does this reliably." Closing that gap is the actual engineering challenge. Prompt design is not a soft skill — it's one of the most consequential decisions you make when deploying any LLM into production.

Data / BI / Career
Power BI in Aged Care: What Building Real Dashboards Taught Me About Data
The most humbling thing about building Power BI dashboards for aged-care organisations is how quickly you learn that clean data is a privilege, not a default. Building billing analytics on top of Procura — extracting meaningful trends, occupancy rates, and subsidy reconciliation — meant wrestling with inconsistent date formats, missing identifiers, and fee categories that had evolved over years of policy change. The dashboard was the easy part. The ETL pipeline took ten times longer and contained ninety percent of the value. A few things I don't see enough in the BI discourse: model first, visualise second — a clean star schema will save hours of debugging DAX later. Learn time intelligence functions and USERELATIONSHIP, not just SUM and CALCULATE. And build for the cognitive load of someone with forty other things to do — fewer visuals, not more. SA's aged-care sector faces growing compliance reporting pressure post-Royal Commission. Organisations investing in BI infrastructure now will be better positioned as that pressure intensifies. For data professionals in Adelaide, this is one of the clearest paths to work with both technical depth and direct social impact.
